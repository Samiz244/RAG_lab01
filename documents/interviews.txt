THE UNOFFICIAL GUIDE TO IVY LEAGUE ADMISSIONS ADVICE FROM ADMITTED STUDENTS
Topic: Interviews
============================================================

This document compiles advice, reflections, and insights on the college admissions interview process shared by students admitted to Ivy League institutions, as well as official guidance from university admissions offices, accounts from alumni interviewers, and publicly available student reflections. Sources include official admissions FAQ pages, student-authored blog posts from admitted students, the Princeton Alumni Weekly, and admissions guidance from Dartmouth, Yale, Harvard, and Princeton. All advice is paraphrased or summarized from publicly available content. No advice has been fabricated.

============================================================

POLICY OVERVIEW: HOW INTERVIEWS WORK ACROSS THE IVY LEAGUE

Interview practices vary significantly across the eight Ivy League schools. As of the 2025-2026 cycle, the landscape is as follows:

Harvard: Offers interviews at the discretion of the Admissions Committee, based in part on alumni availability in the applicant's geographic area. Nearly 10,000 alumni volunteers conduct interviews globally, but most areas cannot interview all applicants. Harvard stresses that not receiving an interview is not a negative signal and applications receive full evaluation without one. The Committee may also schedule interviews when it wants more information about a specific applicant.

Yale: All interviews are formally evaluative. Yale's Alumni Schools Committee (ASC) conducts interviews wherever local alumni volunteers are available. Yale also uses current seniors as interviewers who conduct virtual interviews with selected applicants regardless of location. Applicants cannot request an interview and should not contact the ASC directly. Interviews are not required, and applicants not offered one will not be disadvantaged.

Princeton: Aspires to interview as many applicants as possible through its large alumni volunteer network. Princeton tries to ensure every applicant has access to an interview, though alumni availability determines who is reached. Interviews are 30 to 45 minutes. Princeton instructs its alumni interviewers to think of their role as more akin to an ambassador or reporter than a judge.

Dartmouth: Conducts alumni interviews as part of the admissions process. Both informative and evaluative, the interview report is added to the applicant file and reviewed alongside other materials. Dartmouth tries to interview as many applicants as possible. Interviews last 30 to 45 minutes and take place in person or virtually.

Columbia: Discontinued its alumni interview program entirely as of the 2023-2024 admissions cycle, citing an inability to offer interviews to the vast majority of applicants.

Brown: Replaced formal alumni interviews with an optional two-minute video introduction for applicants, in the interest of equity of experience across all applicants.

UPenn: Renamed alumni interviews as alumni conversations and designated them as non-evaluative beginning with the 2023-2024 cycle. As of 2025-2026, Penn discontinued alumni conversations entirely, citing record application volume and a pivot toward mentorship programming for admitted students.

Cornell: Does not offer formal evaluative interviews for most undergraduate programs. Cornell offers applicants an optional informal, non-evaluative conversation with an alumnus to ask questions about the university.

Entry 1

Source:
Harvard College, "What to Expect After You Apply" (official admissions guidance)
https://college.harvard.edu/guides/what-expect-after-you-apply

School:
Harvard University

Advice:
Harvard's official guidance on post-application steps describes the interview as something that may or may not happen, based entirely on alumni availability and admissions committee discretion. The application is considered complete without an interview, and the committee states it has sufficient information in most cases to reach a decision without one. Harvard makes two things clear about its interview logistics: it is not possible for applicants to request an interview, and Harvard does not conduct interviews in Cambridge. If an alumnus reaches out to schedule one, the guidance is to accept the invitation. Harvard uses nearly 10,000 alumni volunteers globally, but even this large network cannot reach all applicants, particularly in international regions.

Key Takeaway:
Not receiving a Harvard interview is not a rejection signal. Harvard cannot interview every applicant and bases the assignment on geography and committee needs. If offered an interview, always accept. Do not attempt to arrange one yourself.

Key Takeaway:
The Harvard interview is a conversation designed to confirm that the person behind the application is as real and compelling as the materials suggest. Entry 3

Source:
Veritas Essays blog, "How I Got Into Harvard" (admitted Harvard student's first-person account)
https://veritasessays.org/college-admissions-blog/posts/how-i-got-into-harvard

School:
Harvard University

Advice:
A Harvard student who later accessed their admissions file described learning that their interview and essay were the two factors that distinguished their application and secured their acceptance. The interviewer's written report noted three specific qualities. First, the applicant's structured thinking and organization when answering questions: the student had spent the week before the interview practicing how to walk through their background by telling a coherent story. The interviewer noted this could have come across as rehearsed but was saved by the genuine enthusiasm the student showed. Second, the coherent narrative the applicant built connecting their different high school interests, debate and STEM research, into a single forward-looking set of goals at Harvard, specifically mentioning the Berkman Center for Internet and Policy as a resource directly relevant to their academic plan. Third, the easy flow of conversation: the student had researched the interviewer beforehand and knew she worked in health policy. When she asked about their interest in statistics, the student turned the question back on her and asked how statistics factored into her work. This created a genuine back-and-forth dialogue and kept the interviewer engaged throughout. The overall assessment the interviewer wrote was that this was the most exceptional candidate she had seen in almost a decade of interviewing.

Key Takeaway:
The three qualities that earned the most exceptional rating in this Harvard interview report were structured thinking, a coherent narrative connecting past experiences to specific Harvard resources, and a conversational flow driven by genuine curiosity about the interviewer. Research your interviewer beforehand, turn the interview into a dialogue, and tie your interests to specific Harvard programs.

Entry 4

Source:
CollegeVine blog, "The Harvard Interview: What It's Like, from a Harvard Student" (Lily Calcagnini, Harvard student)
https://blog.collegevine.com/the-harvard-interview-what-its-like-from-a-harvard-student

School:
Harvard University

Advice:
A Harvard student described the interview as approximately 45 minutes of pointed questions about the applicant's interests and experiences as they appeared on the Common App, followed by 15 minutes open for the applicant's questions. Harvard interviewers read the applicant's materials in advance, which means they will not ask for a basic description of activities but rather for reflection on them. The student emphasized that there is no correct answer to these questions as long as the response is genuine, but the interview is not the time to consider for the first time what you learned from an experience. That reflection must happen before. Harvard's interview is sometimes conducted by a panel of two alumni rather than one, which the student described as an advantage rather than a disadvantage: it gives the applicant two perspectives on Harvard's community and two opportunities to ask substantive questions. The student's overall message was that interviewers are alumni who once stood in the same shoes as the applicant.

Key Takeaway:
Harvard interviewers have read your application. They are asking you to reflect, not to describe. Do your reflective thinking before the interview, not during it. A two-person panel is not more intimidating than a one-on-one conversation; treat it as access to two Harvard experiences at once.

Entry 5

Source:
AdmitBeacon.com, "Harvard University Interview: Process, Questions and Tips" (March 2026)
https://www.admitbeacon.com/feeds/blog/harvard-university-interview

School:
Harvard University

Advice:
Analysis of the Harvard interview process noted an important structural detail: unlike some other Ivy League schools, Harvard alumni interviewers do not have access to the applicant's full application file before the meeting. They receive only the applicant's name, contact information, and high school name. This means the Harvard interview is less about confirming or elaborating on what is already in the application and more about discovering the person from scratch. The implication for preparation is significant: applicants should be ready to introduce themselves, explain their interests, and build a coherent picture of who they are without being able to assume the interviewer already knows anything about them. Following the Supreme Court's 2023 ruling on affirmative action, Harvard strictly prohibits alumni interviewers from mentioning an applicant's race, ethnicity, or national origin as part of the written report.

Key Takeaway:
Harvard alumni interviewers arrive knowing almost nothing about you. The interview functions as a fresh introduction, not a follow-up conversation about your application. Be prepared to build your portrait from the ground up in 45 to 60 minutes without relying on the interviewer to already know your story.

Entry 7

Source:
Yale College Undergraduate Admissions, "Interviews for First-Year Applicants" (official page)
https://admissions.yale.edu/interviews

School:
Yale University

Advice:
Yale's official admissions page on interviews states directly that all Yale interviews are evaluative, distinguishing Yale clearly from schools like Harvard where the weight of the interview is more ambiguous. The admissions committee reads interview reports alongside all other application materials. Yale's Alumni Schools Committee conducts interviews in most parts of the United States and in many foreign countries. A small group of current Yale seniors also serve as senior interviewers and conduct virtual interviews with selected applicants regardless of geographic location. Applicants may not request an interview and should not contact the ASC. Single-Choice Early Action interviews are scheduled before Regular Decision interviews. Not receiving an interview does not disadvantage an applicant, but declining one when offered is inadvisable.

Key Takeaway:
Yale interviews are explicitly evaluative and the report is reviewed as part of the formal file. This distinguishes Yale from schools where the interview carries lighter weight. Declining a Yale interview invitation is visible to the admissions committee and sends a negative signal.

CollegeVine blog, "Yale Interview: What It's Like and My Experience" (Michelle Foley, Yale admit)
https://blog.collegevine.com/yale-interview-what-its-like

School:
Yale University

Advice:
A Yale admit described her interview experience in detail. Her interviewer, a Yale alumna named Heather, opened by telling her the interview would be very casual and more like a conversation. The discussion ranged broadly, touching on education systems, favorite teachers, what made their teaching effective, and the student's art practice. When the student mentioned she liked to paint, the interviewer was particularly engaged, noting that artistically inclined students who are also academically invested are unusual at Yale. The student showed her interviewer her art Instagram page, and the interviewer shared a favorite abstract painter of her own. One lesson the student drew from the experience: she got too casual at one point when sharing an amusing anecdote about an English teacher, and the interviewer did not respond well to it. The anecdote was entertaining but not interesting in the way the interview setting requires. The student's core advice was to be yourself, but to know your audience, because the interviewer is older and more experienced, and the interview is the place to show maturity and self-awareness.

Key Takeaway:
Yale interviews really are conversational, but casual does not mean anything goes. The interviewer is still evaluating your maturity, self-awareness, and genuine engagement with ideas. Sharing entertaining anecdotes that are not substantively interesting wastes the time and goodwill that should be directed toward showing who you really are.

Entry 11

Source:
Princeton Alumni Weekly, "The Alumni Interview Endures" (in-depth feature article, March/April 2024)
https://paw.princeton.edu/article/alumni-interview-endures

School:
Princeton University

Advice:
Princeton's Dean of Admission Karen Richardson described what alumni interview write-ups do for the admissions file. Admissions officers do not have the chance to sit down with applicants themselves, and the alumni write-up brings the story to life in ways the written application cannot. It can confirm or disprove the narrative visible in the essays and recommendation letters, and helps the committee figure out how a particular student might contribute to the dynamic community already at Princeton. The article also profiled Fred Frelinghuysen, an interviewer of seventeen years, who described his approach as trying to get a sense of what the university didn't have in an applicant's written application: the numbers and recommendation letters were already there; what he wanted to understand was what this person was actually like. Robert Bernstein, a Princeton alumnus who was deeply affected by his own interview and later became an interviewer himself, described the most memorable part of his own experience as the authenticity and sincerity of the questions and the genuine interest his interviewer showed in him as a person.

Key Takeaway:
Princeton interviews serve to bring the written application to life and to surface qualities not visible on the page. Alumni interviewers approach the conversation not as judges but as reporters trying to capture who a student is beyond their credentials. The best interviews leave both parties with a sense of genuine human connection.

Princeton's interviewers are formally asked to assess three things: personal qualities, unusual contextual information not visible in the file, and intellectual curiosity. Advice:

Entry 24

Source:
AdmissionSight.com, "Yale Interview Guide: Sample Questions and Tips" (December 2024)
https://admissionsight.com/does-yale-interview-everyone/

School:
Yale University

Advice:
Analysis of the Yale interview process offered practical guidance on the most common question Yale applicants face: what does receiving or not receiving an interview mean? The analysis confirmed that Yale prioritizes interviews for students for whom the admissions committee needs more information. Not receiving an interview does not hurt an applicant's chances; many successful applicants are never interviewed. If offered an interview, Yale strongly encourages accepting. The analysis also emphasized that the interview is a genuine opportunity to share leadership, creativity, and problem-solving experiences that might not be fully visible in the written application, and that Yale interviewers are specifically looking for evidence of genuine intellectual curiosity and authentic engagement with the things the applicant claims to care about.

Key Takeaway:
Being offered a Yale interview means the admissions committee wants more information about you, which is a signal of serious consideration but not a guarantee of admission. Not being offered one is not a negative signal. If offered, always accept. Come prepared to demonstrate genuine passion for the activities and academic subjects you have described in your application.

Entry 25

Source:
Leland, "Princeton Interview Guide: Questions, Expert Tips and How to Prepare" (June 2025)
https://www.joinleland.com/library/a/navigating-the-princeton-university-interview-questions-tips-and-insights

School:
Princeton University

Advice:
Expert guidance on preparing for the Princeton alumni interview identified the most common strategic mistakes applicants make. The first is speaking in generalities about why they want to attend Princeton, using statements like wanting a strong education in a beautiful setting or being impressed by the alumni network, which could apply to dozens of schools. Princeton is looking for students who have thought deeply about why Princeton specifically, and who can connect that specificity to their intellectual passions and personal values. The guidance recommended referencing unique Princeton programs such as the junior paper and senior thesis requirement, the Bridge Year program, the Lewis Center for the Arts, or specific residential college traditions rather than generic prestige markers. The second mistake is rattling off a dozen prepared questions like a checklist at the end. The guidance recommended picking two to three questions that arise naturally from the conversation and from the alumnus's background, asking with genuine curiosity, and following up on their answers rather than jumping immediately to the next question.

Key Takeaway:
Generic answers to why Princeton are among the most common interview mistakes. Princeton-specific knowledge of programs, traditions, and culture signals that your interest is genuine and researched, not brand-driven. Two or three thoughtful, natural questions are always better than a prepared list of ten.

SUMMARY OF RECURRING THEMES ACROSS ALL ENTRIES

These themes appeared consistently across multiple Ivy League school perspectives and admitted student reflections:

1. The interview landscape is school-specific and has shifted dramatically. Yale and Dartmouth maintain explicitly evaluative interviews with written reports. Harvard and Princeton interview selectively based on availability and committee needs. Columbia has eliminated interviews. Brown uses a video introduction. Penn has ended its alumni conversation program. Cornell does not offer evaluative interviews for most programs. Know each school's current policy.

2. Not receiving an interview is not a negative signal at any school that cannot interview all applicants. Harvard, Yale, and Princeton are all explicit that missing an interview does not disadvantage an application. Applications receive full evaluation without one.

3. Always accept an interview invitation when offered. Declining a Yale, Harvard, Princeton, or Dartmouth interview is visible and sends an unintentionally negative signal.

4. The interview is conversational, not an interrogation. Every Ivy League school describes its interview as a conversation, and alumni interviewers are trained to set a relaxed tone. Treat it as a dialogue, not a performance.

5. Intellectual curiosity is the single most consistently named quality interviewers are asked to assess. Princeton's formal interviewer guide specifically asks about it. Harvard interview reports highlight it by name. Yale interviewers describe it as a core criterion. Prepare to discuss specific academic areas, works, or projects with genuine enthusiasm.

6. Research your interviewer beforehand when possible. The Harvard student who earned the most exceptional rating in a decade did so partly by researching the interviewer's career in health policy and turning questions around into a genuine back-and-forth. This transforms an interview into a dialogue.

7. The questions you ask matter as much as the questions you answer. Yale's official guidance explicitly notes that interviewers learn from the interesting, thoughtful questions applicants bring. Generic questions that can be answered by reading the school's website are wasted opportunities.

8. Specificity beats generality in every answer. The most common piece of advice across all schools was to move from broad statements to specific examples, specific programs, specific experiences, and specific impacts.
