POLICY OVERVIEW: THE RETURN TO TESTING AT THE IVY LEAGUE

Before individual entries, a note on the rapidly shifting policy landscape as of 2025-2026:

Six of the eight Ivy League schools now require standardized test scores for first-year applicants entering the Class of 2031 (applying in fall 2026). Harvard reinstated the SAT/ACT requirement in April 2024. Yale adopted a test-flexible policy in February 2024, requiring scores but accepting SAT, ACT, AP, or IB results. Dartmouth, Brown, Cornell, and Penn reinstated standardized testing for Fall 2025 applicants. Princeton remains test-optional for one final cycle (2026-2027 applicants) but announced in October 2025 that it will require testing starting with the 2027-2028 cycle. Columbia is the only Ivy League school with a permanent test-optional policy.

All policy citations in this document draw from each institution's own announcements and published Common Data Set reports.

Entry 3

Source:
Harvard Gazette, "Harvard Announces Return to Required Testing" (April 2024)
https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2024/04/harvard-announces-return-to-required-testing/

School:
Harvard University

Advice:
When announcing the return to required testing, Harvard's Dean of the Faculty of Arts and Sciences Hopi Hoekstra explained the reasoning directly. Standardized tests provide information that is predictive of success in college and beyond for all students regardless of their background and life experience. She noted that the research cited included a study by Harvard economists Raj Chetty, David Deming, and colleague John Friedman, using data from more than 400 institutions and about 3.5 million undergraduates annually. That research showed that standardized test scores, unlike high school grades alone, were highly predictive of post-college success. Chetty's own comment on the findings was notable: he acknowledged that students from higher-income families often have greater access to test prep resources, but noted that other admissions measures such as recommendation letters, extracurriculars, and essays were even more prone to such wealth-correlated biases. Requiring test scores was therefore likely to make the admissions process more meritocratic, not less.

Key Takeaway:
The faculty research cited by Harvard found that test scores are better predictors of college and post-college success than other application components, and that essays, recommendations, and extracurriculars show greater correlations with family income than test scores do. This research fundamentally reframed how Harvard thought about the equity implications of test-optional policies.

Yale University

Advice:
Yale's official letter to school counselors explaining the new test-flexible policy detailed how it would work in practice. Starting with the 2024-2025 admissions cycle, all applicants were asked to choose which scores they wanted considered from four options: SAT, ACT, AP, or IB. Yale also addressed students who lacked access to any testing: applicants at schools that did not offer AP or IB courses would not be penalized for being unable to submit those alternatives. Yale's stated goal was to empower applicants to put their best foot forward, recognizing that no single test is a perfect or one-size-fits-all demonstration of college readiness.

Key Takeaway:
Yale's test-flexible policy is not a loophole but a genuine recognition that different students have access to different types of rigorous academic assessment. Students who do well on AP exams in subjects related to their intended area of study now have a legitimate and sometimes stronger way to demonstrate academic preparation than a single standardized test date.

Coffin specifically described cases where students from high schools with limited context visible to admissions officers could use a test score as a clear, objective signal of preparation that the transcript alone could not provide as clearly. Elite private schools have guidance counselors who write detailed letters explaining the rigor of their school's curriculum; students from under-resourced public schools often lack that context. A test score fills a critical contextual gap for those students.

Key Takeaway:
For students from schools where admissions officers have limited context about curriculum rigor, a test score provides an objective signal of academic preparation that no other component can substitute for as effectively. Students from under-resourced schools who have scores at or above the school's local-context threshold should almost always submit.

SUMMARY OF RECURRING THEMES ACROSS ALL ENTRIES

These themes appeared consistently across multiple Ivy League school perspectives and institutional research findings:

1. The testing landscape has fundamentally shifted. As of 2026, six of eight Ivy League schools require standardized test scores. Only Columbia has a permanent test-optional policy. Princeton's test-optional window closes with the 2027-2028 cycle. Students should check each school's current published policy before making decisions.

2. Scores are evaluated in context, not in isolation. Every school, most explicitly Harvard, Yale, and Dartmouth, evaluates scores relative to the local norms of a student's high school and community. A score below the published median can still strengthen an application if it represents genuine achievement within the student's educational context.

3. Lower-income students made a systematic error by withholding scores. The central finding that drove Ivy League reinstatements was that lower-income and first-generation students were disproportionately withholding scores that, when contextualized, would have helped them gain admission. Students from under-resourced schools who have earned competitive scores should submit them.

4. Test scores are more predictive than high school GPA. Institutional research at Dartmouth, Harvard, and Brown found that standardized test scores predicted college academic performance more reliably than GPA, partly because grade inflation has compressed GPAs at the top of the distribution. A 4.0 GPA no longer reliably distinguishes students from one another; test scores still do.

5. The score ranges have shifted upward significantly. The 25th percentile at Harvard, Yale, and Princeton climbed roughly 50 points since 2018-2019, driven by the self-selection of high scorers during test-optional years. Students should benchmark against current published data, not pre-pandemic norms.

6. A score at or above the 75th percentile is an active asset; below the 25th percentile is a meaningful weakness. The middle-50 percent framework is the most practical tool for understanding where your score positions you at each target school. Above the 75th, scores work for you. Below the 25th, scores work against you and require compensation from other parts of the application.

7. Superscoring is widely available and should be used strategically. Most Ivy League schools superscore the SAT. Students who are close to their target range on one section but further away on another can make efficient gains by retaking and targeting the weaker section, rather than attempting to improve both sections simultaneously.

8. A perfect score does not guarantee admission. Every Ivy League school rejects students with perfect 1600 SAT and 36 ACT scores. Scores are a necessary threshold, not a sufficient cause of admission. Above the threshold, every other component of the application competes for the final decision.
