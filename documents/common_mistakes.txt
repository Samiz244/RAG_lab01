The Unofficial Guide to Ivy League Admissions Advice from Admitted Students
TOPIC: Common Mistakes

This document compiles advice, experiences, and reflections from students admitted to Ivy League schools, drawn from public discussions, student blogs, YouTube interviews, and admissions-focused online communities. Each entry is sourced from a publicly available post, video, or blog. All advice is paraphrased or summarized from the original sources.

---

Entry 1

Source:
The College Panda (thecollegepanda.com) - Interview compilation featuring Greg Smith (Princeton/UPenn admitted)
https://thecollegepanda.com/admissions-advice-from-students-who-have-overcome-the-odds/

School:
Princeton / UPenn

Advice:
One of the biggest mistakes applicants make is treating their extracurricular list like a checklist of impressive-sounding activities rather than a genuine reflection of who they are. Greg Smith described how, during his sophomore year, he was involved in a large number of random clubs and activities in an effort to look well-rounded. He eventually realized this was the wrong approach entirely. He cut activities that did not connect to his core passions, dropping things like track and standard volunteer work, and instead doubled down on Chinese language study and started a community service club that allowed student musicians to perform publicly. This reorientation made his application far more distinctive.

Key Takeaway:
Colleges are not looking for a well-rounded individual so much as a well-rounded class. Focusing on one or two genuine passions and demonstrating real depth in those areas is far more effective than padding your application with disconnected activities. The mistake is doing activities for the application rather than doing them for yourself.

Entry 2

Source:
The College Panda (thecollegepanda.com) - Interview with Michael (Harvard admitted)
https://thecollegepanda.com/admissions-advice-from-students-who-have-overcome-the-odds/

School:
Harvard

Advice:
Michael emphasized that one of the most common mistakes students make is treating their college application essay as a generic document rather than something deeply personal and identifiable. His rule was that if you erased all the names from a collection of essays and shuffled them together, a reader should still be able to pick out your essay as yours based on voice and content alone. He also warned against students being too precious or embarrassed about their essays and refusing to show them to others. He said the fear of sharing was itself a mistake because feedback from teachers, friends, and parents was essential to knowing whether the essay was landing the way it should.

Key Takeaway:
Your essay should be so distinctively yours that it could not belong to anyone else. Keeping your essay private out of embarrassment prevents you from getting critical feedback that could make it significantly stronger. Show it to people whose reaction you trust.

Entry 3

Source:
The College Panda (thecollegepanda.com) - Interview with Kat (Columbia admitted)
https://thecollegepanda.com/admissions-advice-from-students-who-have-overcome-the-odds/

School:
Columbia

Advice:
Kat recommended that students avoid joining a large number of clubs simply for the sake of having a longer list of activities. She found through her own experience that having two or three extracurriculars where you earn leadership roles and demonstrate sustained commitment was far more compelling than twelve clubs with no depth. She also stressed the importance of figuring out what uniquely defines you and presenting that clearly, noting that applicants often overlook their most interesting qualities because those qualities feel ordinary to them personally.

Key Takeaway:
Depth beats breadth in extracurriculars. The common mistake of joining every club available results in an application that looks scattered rather than focused. Sustained involvement in fewer activities, combined with leadership and meaningful accomplishments, tells a much clearer and more persuasive story.

Entry 4

Source:
The College Panda (thecollegepanda.com) - Interview with Anna (Cornell admitted)
https://thecollegepanda.com/admissions-advice-from-students-who-have-overcome-the-odds/

School:
Cornell

Advice:
Anna's story is a useful counterpoint to students who assume straight A's are required. Her grades were far from perfect when she entered high school, but she demonstrated a clear upward trend through junior year. She emphasized that an upward trend is a story admissions officers can tell on your behalf inside the committee room. She also cautioned students against underestimating the essay and the interview. Several of her classmates who got into Cornell believed the interview was a decisive factor in their admissions. Treating the interview as a mere formality is a serious mistake.

Key Takeaway:
An upward academic trend can be more compelling than a flat but perfect GPA. Do not treat the interview as unimportant. Students who under-prepare for the interview or see it as a box to check often miss one of their best opportunities to make a personal impression.

Entry 5

Source:
Mistakes Were Made (Substack) - Ari Shtein, Yale and Columbia admitted student
https://mistakesweremade.substack.com/p/my-college-application-essay

School:
Yale / Columbia

Advice:
Ari Shtein, who was admitted to both Yale and Columbia, offered pointed commentary on what sank a widely-discussed application in 2024. The applicant in question had impressive credentials including a business generating tens of millions in revenue, a strong GPA, and solid test scores, but was rejected everywhere. Shtein and others who analyzed the situation pointed to the essay as the primary problem. The essay came across as self-congratulatory and framed the applicant as someone with an inflated sense of his own importance. Shtein noted that the first rule of college application essays is not to come across as arrogant. He also listed other common essay pitfalls: starting nearly every sentence with I, inserting SAT vocabulary words to appear smart, ending with a tidy life lesson that sounds formulaic, and writing about a deceased grandparent as the central subject.

Key Takeaway:
Even extraordinary credentials cannot overcome an essay that makes an admissions reader dislike you. Sounding arrogant, formulaic, or inauthentic in your essay is one of the most reliable ways to hurt an otherwise strong application. The essay is your personality on paper, and personality matters enormously.

Entry 6

Source:
Harvard College Student Stories Blog (college.harvard.edu) - Braeden, Class of 2021
https://college.harvard.edu/student-life/student-stories/my-admissions-journey

School:
Harvard

Advice:
Braeden reflected on his admissions process and identified applying to twelve schools as a mistake. He paid application fees out of pocket and watched his savings dwindle applying to schools ranked highly on websites but schools he was not genuinely passionate about. He also said that applying to schools for other people's expectations rather than your own genuine interest was a fundamental error. He felt caught between the schools others expected him to want and the schools that actually suited him.

Key Takeaway:
Applying to schools because they rank well or because others expect you to, rather than because you genuinely want to attend, is a waste of time, money, and emotional energy. Build a school list based on real fit. Having too many schools on your list can also dilute the quality of your supplemental essays.

Entry 7

Source:
Harvard College Student Stories Blog (college.harvard.edu) - Harvard student, "My Top Tips for Applying to Harvard"
https://college.harvard.edu/student-life/student-stories/my-top-tips-applying-harvard

School:
Harvard

Advice:
This Harvard student described going through many essay prompts before finding the right approach. The mistake was trying to write what the admissions office wanted to hear rather than what was authentically true for the applicant. Every time the student tried to write toward an imagined reader's preferences, the essay felt hollow. Once the student started writing about experiences that were genuinely most important to them personally, the essay improved dramatically and revealed a clear sense of who they were.

Key Takeaway:
One of the most common essay mistakes is writing for an imaginary ideal reader rather than writing honestly about yourself. Admissions officers read tens of thousands of essays and can tell immediately when a student is performing rather than sharing. Authenticity is both harder and more effective than calculated impression management.

Entry 8

Source:
IvyWise KnowledgeBase (ivywise.com) - Former MIT admissions officer perspective
https://www.ivywise.com/ivywise-knowledgebase/10-college-application-mistakes-to-avoid/

School:
Multiple Ivy League / MIT

Advice:
A former MIT admissions officer writing for IvyWise described several mistakes that sent applications to the rejection pile quickly. One major error was students leaving out important personal context. Applicants from demanding home environments, who worked part-time jobs to support their families or cared for younger siblings, sometimes failed to mention this context at all, leaving admissions readers to compare them unfairly against students with far more resources and free time. Another common mistake was writing one-sided essays that focused entirely on what the college could do for the applicant without addressing what the applicant would contribute. A third mistake was lacking vision: students who said they wanted to attend a prestigious school in order to get a good job after graduation made little impression compared to students who articulated a compelling direction for their future.

Key Takeaway:
Failing to provide context about your life circumstances is a significant missed opportunity. Admissions committees cannot give you credit for overcoming obstacles they do not know about. Additionally, essays that are all about receiving rather than giving signal a transactional mindset that does not impress selective schools.

Entry 9

Source:
IvyWise KnowledgeBase (ivywise.com) - Admissions counselor, formerly of University of Pennsylvania
https://www.ivywise.com/ivywise-knowledgebase/10-college-application-mistakes-to-avoid/

School:
UPenn / Multiple

Advice:
Former admissions counselors noted that one of the most damaging and embarrassing mistakes an applicant can make is submitting a Why This School essay that mentions the wrong school's name. This happens when students copy and paste from one supplement to another and forget to change the school name. Being rejected from MIT because your essay mentions Harvard is entirely avoidable and signals carelessness. Similarly, submitting a generic Why This School essay that could apply to any institution shows the admissions office that you did not take the time to research the school seriously. The essay should contain specific details about programs, professors, courses, clubs, and campus communities that attract you.

Key Takeaway:
Copying supplemental essays without updating school-specific details is one of the most preventable admissions mistakes. A generic Why This School essay communicates a lack of genuine interest in the institution. Specificity demonstrates that you have done your homework and that your interest is real.

Entry 10

Source:
AdmitSee Blog (admitsee.com) - Princeton applicant reflection
https://www.admitsee.com/blog/college-application-regrets-what-id-do-differently

School:
Princeton

Advice:
A student who had applied to Princeton shared that they were so fixated on one particular feature of the school, a tuition-free gap year program, that they did not take the time to explore other schools with open minds. By the time they realized they might actually love Yale-NUS in Singapore, it was too late to make a compelling case to their family about attending. This tunnel vision on a single aspect of a single school narrowed their options unnecessarily and left them with regret about how they had approached building their college list.

Key Takeaway:
Fixating too heavily on one school or one feature of a school limits your ability to make sound decisions. Build your list with genuine curiosity about multiple institutions. Students who go into the process obsessively focused on a single target school often miss excellent alternatives that might actually be better fits.

Entry 11

Source:
AdmitSee Blog (admitsee.com) - General advice from admitted students
https://www.admitsee.com/blog/college-application-regrets-what-id-do-differently

School:
Multiple

Advice:
A commonly shared piece of advice from students who ultimately got into their target schools was that they waited too long to start their essays. Nearly every student interviewed said they wished they had started earlier. Beginning the brainstorming process in the spring of junior year, rather than scrambling in the fall of senior year, allows enough time to write multiple drafts, get feedback, and let ideas evolve naturally. Students who rushed their essays in the final weeks before deadlines consistently felt their applications were weaker because of it.

Key Takeaway:
Starting essays late is one of the most consistent and avoidable mistakes in the Ivy League application process. Rushed essays show. The strongest applications reflect weeks or months of iterative writing and revision, not a last-minute sprint.

Entry 12

Source:
The College Panda (thecollegepanda.com) - Summary of advice from multiple admitted students
https://thecollegepanda.com/admissions-advice-from-students-who-have-overcome-the-odds/

School:
Multiple Ivy League

Advice:
One student quoted in the College Panda compilation said she made the mistake of not sharing her essays with anyone because they were too personal and she felt protective of them. She later called this a significant error. Getting feedback does not mean accepting every suggestion, but it is the only way to know how an essay lands with a real reader. An essay that feels deeply personal and powerful to its author may read as self-indulgent or confusing to someone who does not know you.

Key Takeaway:
Refusing to share your essays out of protectiveness or embarrassment is a mistake. Outside readers, especially those who know you well, can tell you whether the essay actually sounds like you and whether it communicates what you intend. This feedback is essential and cannot be replicated by re-reading your own work.

Entry 13

Source:
Patch.com / College Essay Adviser Observations - Feedback from Ivy League admissions officers relayed by Dr. Paul Lowe
https://patch.com/new-york/rye/college-application-essays--avoid-the-stress_39867aa7

School:
Multiple Ivy League

Advice:
Admissions officers at Ivy League schools reported that some of the most common essay failures included essays that sounded as though they were written by the applicant's parents, essays that were verbose and said little of substance, essays that were formulaic and followed a predictable structure, and essays that seemed copied from online samples or published books of successful essays. One admissions officer stated that if an essay was notably weak, both first and second readers would likely flag it and factor it negatively into their recommendation. A badly written essay can definitely hurt an otherwise strong application.

Key Takeaway:
Essays that read as parent-written, formulaic, or modeled too closely on published examples are immediately recognizable to admissions readers. Using AI or over-relying on a counselor's rewrites has similar consequences. Your own authentic voice, even if less polished, is far more effective than a technically perfect essay that sounds like someone else.

Entry 14

Source:
Patch.com / College Essay Advice - Multiple workshop accounts from admissions advisers
https://patch.com/new-york/rye/college-application-essays--avoid-the-stress_39867aa7

School:
Multiple Ivy League

Advice:
A repeated theme from admissions advisers who had worked with Ivy League applicants was the mistake of trying too hard to sound intellectual. Using a thesaurus to insert elevated vocabulary into an essay does not make a student seem smarter; it makes the writing feel stilted and inauthentic. Admissions officers who read thousands of essays develop a strong sense of when a student is writing naturally versus when they are performing intelligence. Words like plethora, myriad, and copious were flagged specifically as signals of this kind of overreach. Similarly, trying too hard to appear creative by writing highly stylized or abstract essays, comparing one's life to abstract concepts or unusual metaphors, tends to produce essays void of actual personality.

Key Takeaway:
Performing intelligence through vocabulary is a well-known and ineffective strategy. Admissions readers want to understand who you are, not be impressed by your knowledge of synonyms. Write clearly and specifically rather than cleverly and abstractly.

Entry 15

Source:
Patch.com / College Essay Advice - Multiple workshop accounts
https://patch.com/michigan/macomb/bp--applying-to-an-ivy-league-university

School:
Multiple

Advice:
Another frequently cited mistake was writing about a deceased grandparent or another family member as the central subject of the personal statement. The essay prompt is asking about you, not about someone else. Admissions readers see thousands of these essays every cycle. A grandparent's remarkable life does not reveal what makes the applicant distinctive. Unless the subject of a family member is used as a specific lens to examine something deeply personal about the student's own character, values, or development, this topic almost always backfires.

Key Takeaway:
Essays centered on a deceased relative or another person's qualities are extremely common and rarely effective. The application is about you, and any person you write about should serve as a vehicle for illuminating something specific and revealing about yourself. If removing the person from the essay would leave nothing distinctive, the essay needs a different approach.

Entry 16

Source:
Business Insider / College Admissions Expert Commentary - Steve Gardner, Harvard Summer School instructor
https://new-test-origin.patch.com/connecticut/bethwood/college-application-essay-help-nodx

School:
Yale / Princeton / Harvard

Advice:
Steve Gardner, who has instructed at Harvard and has tracked admissions outcomes closely, challenged a widely held assumption that class rank and GPA are the primary deciding factors at Ivy League schools. One student he guided was admitted to Stanford despite being ranked in the 91st percentile at her school, not at the top of her class. Several classmates ranked above her were rejected. Another student whose essay was about busking in Europe was admitted to Yale, Stanford, and Princeton. Gardner's observation was that students who spend all their energy optimizing their academic record while neglecting the narrative and human elements of their application are making a strategic mistake.

Key Takeaway:
Assuming that a near-perfect GPA or a top class rank is sufficient for Ivy League admission is one of the most common and costly misconceptions. Admissions officers at highly selective schools are building a class, not ranking applicants by GPA. An authentic, revealing application can outperform a technically superior but personality-free one.

Entry 17

Source:
Applying to College (Patch.com) - High school senior blog post, applicant to Cornell, Yale, Brown, Princeton
https://patch.com/michigan/macomb/bp--applying-to-an-ivy-league-university

School:
Cornell / Yale / Brown / Princeton

Advice:
A high school senior who was actively applying to several Ivy League schools noted that one underappreciated mistake is failing to research each school's specific test score requirements and submission processes. Some students assume SAT or ACT scores are automatically sent when in fact they must be ordered separately through College Board or ACT directly. Missing this step can delay or compromise an application. The student also noted that many applicants underestimate the weight of the supplemental essays, treating them as secondary to the Common App essay, when in fact they may be the primary mechanism by which admissions offices evaluate fit with their specific institution.

Key Takeaway:
Logistical mistakes like failing to submit test scores through the proper channels, or treating supplements as less important than the personal statement, can derail an otherwise strong application. Ivy League supplements often ask specific questions about fit and contribution that deserve as much care and specificity as the main essay.

Entry 18

Source:
Ivy League Admissions Discussions - Widely reported in A2C and College Confidential communities

School:
Multiple

Advice:
A concern that surfaces repeatedly in admissions discussions is students who apply to Ivy League schools without applying to any realistic safety schools. The mentality that one of the eight schools must surely accept you is a dangerous one. Students who apply exclusively to elite institutions and receive nothing but rejections have no backup, and all the time invested in those applications produces nothing actionable. A related mistake is applying to schools solely because of prestige and rankings without doing any actual research on whether the school is the right fit academically, socially, or geographically.

Key Takeaway:
A college list with no financial or academic safety schools is a gamble that frequently backfires. Applying to Ivy League schools should never crowd out thoughtful applications to schools where you are likely to be admitted. Building a balanced list is itself a skill and an important part of application strategy.

Entry 19

Source:
Ivy Coach Blog (ivycoach.com) - Commentary from former Ivy admissions readers
https://www.ivycoach.com/the-ivy-coach-blog/college-admissions/college-admission-essays/

School:
Multiple Ivy League

Advice:
Former admissions readers for Ivy League schools described the specific mistake of bragging in the personal statement. Applicants who structured their essays as a list of accomplishments, framed as proof that they deserve admission, produced essays that admissions readers found unappealing. The essay is not a resume in paragraph form. Starting most sentences with I and cataloguing achievements without reflection or depth tends to come across as selling rather than sharing. One counselor put it directly: bragging is a reliable way to make an admissions officer want to recommend denial.

Key Takeaway:
The personal statement is not the place to list your accomplishments; your resume and activities section do that. An essay that reads as a sales pitch for why the admissions office should admit you misunderstands the purpose of the essay entirely. Reflection, vulnerability, and specificity are more effective than achievement summaries.

Entry 20

Source:
Admissions Decisions Substack (admissionsdecisions.substack.com) - Observations from students and counselors
https://admissionsdecisions.substack.com/p/5-more-things-to-avoid-in-college

School:
Multiple

Advice:
Two technical mistakes that appear in discussions consistently are failing to proofread essays and writing a response to an essay prompt that does not actually answer the question asked. Some students write a general essay they like and then look for a prompt it loosely fits, rather than writing to the specific prompt. Admissions readers notice this immediately. Similarly, typos and grammatical errors undermine an otherwise polished application. Spellcheck catches some errors but does not catch homophones, missing words, or sentences that are grammatically correct but logically incomplete.

Key Takeaway:
Writing a general essay and hunting for a prompt it fits is the wrong approach and results in essays that often do not address the question. Read each prompt carefully and write specifically to it. Proofread every essay multiple times and have at least two other people read for errors before submission.

Entry 21

Source:
College Confidential / A2C Community Discussions - Widely repeated advice from admitted students

School:
Multiple Ivy League

Advice:
A recurring theme in admissions discussions from students who were admitted to Ivy League schools is the misconception that prestigious summer programs, such as paid university programs or expensive pre-college experiences, automatically boost an application. Multiple admitted students noted that these programs are often treated by admissions officers as resume padding unless the student can articulate specifically what they learned, how it influenced their thinking, or how it connected to their authentic interests. A student who attended a Harvard summer program and writes about it as a credential is less compelling than a student who spent that summer on a self-directed project that grew naturally from their interests.

Key Takeaway:
Expensive or prestigious-sounding summer programs do not carry the weight many applicants believe they do. Admissions readers are experienced at distinguishing between students who pursued experiences out of genuine interest and students whose parents enrolled them in programs to manufacture a credential. Authenticity of motivation matters more than the prestige of the activity.

Entry 22

Source:
Harvard College Student Stories Blog (college.harvard.edu) - Admitted student reflection
https://college.harvard.edu/student-life/student-stories/what-happened-when-i-got-accepted-harvard

School:
Harvard

Advice:
A Harvard student reflected honestly on the experience of worrying that they had been admitted by mistake and that attending would expose them as a fraud. This experience, often called impostor syndrome, is very common among admitted Ivy League students and suggests something important about the application process: the students who do get in often do not fit the fantasy image of a perfect, polished, supremely confident applicant. The mistake many applicants make is performing confidence and perfection rather than showing genuine curiosity, uncertainty, and growth. Admissions officers are reading for intellectual engagement and human dimension, not a flawless presentation.

Key Takeaway:
Applicants who craft a self-image of perfect achievement and certainty in their essays often come across as less compelling than those who show genuine intellectual curiosity and the capacity for growth. The admissions process rewards humans, not performances. Trying to appear perfect can make you seem less interesting than being honestly yourself.
