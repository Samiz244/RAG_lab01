THE UNOFFICIAL GUIDE TO IVY LEAGUE ADMISSIONS ADVICE FROM ADMITTED STUDENTS
Topic: Essays
============================================================

This document compiles advice, reflections, and insights on college application essays shared by students who were admitted to Ivy League institutions. Sources include official university student blogs, the Harvard Gazette, Princeton Admission student blogs, college admissions discussion platforms, and publicly available accounts from admitted students. All advice is paraphrased or summarized from publicly available content. No advice has been fabricated.

============================================================

Entry 1

Source:
Harvard Gazette, "How I Wrote My Harvard Essay" (September 6, 2019)
https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2019/09/how-they-wrote-and-rewrote-their-harvard-admissions-essays/

School:
Harvard University

Advice:
Allison Tu, a first-year student from Louisville, Kentucky, described her process as deliberately uninhibited. She stayed up late at night specifically because her lowered inhibitions allowed her to brainstorm without self-criticism. She generated approximately twenty ideas, narrowed them down to five, wrote full drafts of each of those five, and then chose one to revise repeatedly until it felt complete. Her final essay explored the transition from personal independence to interdependence, using her parents' divorce as the catalyst for that growth. The essay was not about the divorce itself but about what she learned from it: how to collaborate, lead others, and depend on a team to accomplish meaningful things in her community.

Key Takeaway:
Removing your internal critic during the brainstorming phase allows for more honest self-reflection. Generating many ideas before selecting one leads to a stronger final essay. Personal growth stories do not need to center on trauma but rather on the insight gained from it.

Harvard University

Advice:
Nick Nocita, from Arlington Heights, Illinois, wrote his Harvard essay about his grandmother, who passed away several years before he applied. He was struck by the inspiration mid-family gathering on Thanksgiving and wrote much of his essay on his phone while his family watched football. His grandmother had only completed a high school education, and seeing what she accomplished within those constraints made him think deeply about what he could do with the opportunity of a prestigious college education. The connection was personal and specific: he was not writing generically about loss but about how one person's limitations reframed his own sense of possibility and ambition.

Key Takeaway:
Essays that emerge from genuine emotion and personal connection, even in unexpected moments, often feel the most authentic. Writing about the influence of a family member works best when the reflection focuses on what the writer learned rather than simply describing the person.

Divya Amirtharaj, from Portland, Oregon, described struggling for weeks with writer's block before finding her subject. Once she identified her topic, however, she completed her draft within a day. Writer's block is normal; the breakthrough often comes from identifying a subject that genuinely captures your personal journey. Two very different essays can coexist in an application, each revealing a distinct and important dimension of who you are.

Harvard University

Advice:
Sophie Clivio, originally from Kingston, Jamaica, took an unconventional structural approach: she wrote her essay as a spoken-word poem rather than a traditional paragraph-based essay. Her subject was the cultural and racial identity shift she experienced when moving from Jamaica to attend boarding school in Milton, Massachusetts. She acknowledged later discovering she had submitted the essay with a typo and an undeleted editorial comment at the bottom. Despite the mistake, she was admitted. Format experimentation can pay off if it reflects your genuine voice and identity. Admissions officers read thousands of traditional essays; one with a distinctive structure and authentic cultural perspective stands out. Perfection in formatting matters less than authenticity in content.

Entry 7

Source:
Harvard College Student Blog, "A First Year's Perspective on Applying to Harvard"
https://college.harvard.edu/student-life/student-stories/first-years-perspective-applying-harvard

School:
Harvard University

Advice:
A Harvard first-year student recommended starting the application process early, specifically getting the administrative and logistical pieces out of the way before the application season rush, so that focus could then shift entirely to essays and written responses. The student stressed getting feedback on personal and supplemental essays from at least one trusted reader, noting that what a writer thinks they are conveying may land differently with an outside reader. However, they cautioned against asking too many people for feedback, as this can lead to a diluted, committee-written essay that no longer sounds like the applicant.

Key Takeaway:
Starting early frees up time and mental energy for the essays, which matter most. One or two trusted readers give useful perspective without overwriting your voice. The essay must still sound like you.

Entry 8

Source:
Princeton Admission Student Blog, "On Applying to College"
Lake Liao, Class of 2027, published September 5, 2024
https://admission.princeton.edu/blogs/applying-college

School:
Princeton University

Advice:
Lake Liao, a Princeton student concentrating in Philosophy, shared five principles that guided their successful application in fall 2022. The first was to know who you are, explaining that college applications require you to articulate a sense of self, and if you can answer questions like what you believe most deeply and what you can talk about for hours without preparation, those answers will translate into a compelling personal narrative. The second was to do your homework about the school, emphasizing that specific and genuine knowledge of a university's professors, research centers, student groups, and academic culture makes a far stronger impression than generic praise of the institution.

Key Takeaway:
Self-knowledge is the foundation of a strong application essay. The more clearly you understand what drives you, the more compelling your writing will be. School-specific research is not optional; it demonstrates genuine interest and seriousness.

Entry 10

Source:
Yale University Admissions Office, "Advice on Putting Together Your Application"
https://admissions.yale.edu/advice-putting-together-your-application

School:
Yale University

Advice:
Yale's guidance, rooted in feedback from its admissions committee, encourages students to write about something that matters to them in their own voice. The office explicitly discourages students from using overly complex vocabulary or convoluted sentence structures to sound impressive. Yale notes that it has read strong essays on completely ordinary topics and weak essays on highly unusual ones, concluding that the perspective and lens through which a student views their topic is far more important than the topic itself. Yale specifically says it does not expect students to fit their entire life story into two short essays; rather, it wants essays that give a real sense of who the person is.

Key Takeaway:
Topic selection matters less than perspective and voice. Impressive vocabulary does not substitute for genuine insight. Yale wants to hear you, not the version of you that you think sounds most impressive.

Entry 11

Source:
Yale Admissions Officer Advice (via Veritas Essays admissions blog, citing Inside the Yale Admissions Office podcast)
https://veritasessays.org/college-admissions-blog/posts/college-admissions-officers-best-college-essay-strategies-for-acceptance

School:
Yale University

Advice:
Yale admissions officers described the role of the personal essay as a kind of glue that binds together the other parts of the application. Rather than functioning as its own isolated showcase, the best essays create coherence across the entire application, making the admissions reader feel they are encountering the same person consistently throughout. This means the essay should connect to and reinforce what appears in the activity list, the recommendations, and the academic record. An essay that introduces a completely disconnected aspect of the applicant without threading back to the rest of the application loses this cohesive power.

Key Takeaway:
Think of the essay as a connective thread, not a standalone performance. When an admissions reader finishes your application and feels they have met one consistent person across every section, that is when the essay has done its job.

SUMMARY OF RECURRING THEMES ACROSS ALL ENTRIES

These themes appeared consistently across multiple Ivy League school perspectives and admitted student reflections:

1. Authenticity over performance. Every source, across every school, emphasized that essays written to impress rather than to reveal genuine self tend to fail. Voice and perspective matter more than topic.

2. Start early. Multiple admitted students and advisors cited beginning the essay process at least one month, often more, before the deadline as a key factor in producing quality work.

3. Specific beats generic. Whether describing a school, an experience, or a goal, specificity consistently outperforms broad statements. Name the professor, the club, the memory, the moment.

4. The essay is not your resume. It should reveal something not visible elsewhere in the application. Repeating accomplishments from the activity list wastes one of the few opportunities for direct communication with an admissions officer.

5. Feedback matters, but not too much. One or two trusted readers improve an essay. Too many readers produce committee writing that no longer sounds like the applicant.

6. Perspective is the essay. Yale, Princeton, and Harvard all stated in different ways that the lens through which you view a topic is more important than the topic itself.

7. Vulnerability combined with growth is powerful. Essays that acknowledge difficulty or failure and then demonstrate genuine learning consistently landed in the strongest category across multiple schools.

8. The essay should connect to the whole application. The best essays function as connective tissue that ties together the academic record, activities, and recommendations into a coherent portrait of one person.
